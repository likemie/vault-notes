**Maximilian Carl Emil Weber** ([/ ˈ v eɪ b ər /](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/English "Help:IPA/English"); German: [\[ˈveːbɐ\]](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Help:IPA/Standard_German "Help:IPA/Standard German") ;[^1] 21 April 1864 – 14 June 1920) was a German [sociologist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology "Sociology"), historian, [jurist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jurist "Jurist"), and [political economist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_economy "Political economy") who was one of the central figures in the development of sociology and the [social sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science") more generally. His ideas continue to influence [social theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_theory "Social theory") and [research](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_research "Social research").

Born in [Erfurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erfurt "Erfurt") in 1864, Weber studied law and history in [Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin "Humboldt University of Berlin"), [Göttingen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen "University of Göttingen"), and [Heidelberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heidelberg_University "Heidelberg University"). After earning his doctorate in law in 1889 and [habilitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habilitation "Habilitation") in 1891, he taught in Berlin, [Freiburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Freiburg "University of Freiburg"), and Heidelberg. He married his cousin [Marianne Schnitger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marianne_Weber "Marianne Weber") two years later. In 1897, he had a breakdown after [his father](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Weber_Sr. "Max Weber Sr.") died following an argument. Weber ceased teaching and travelled until the early 1900s. He recovered and wrote *[The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Protestant_Ethic_and_the_Spirit_of_Capitalism "The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism")*. During the [First World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_World_War "First World War"), he initially supported Germany's war effort but became critical of it and supported democratisation. He also gave the lectures " [Science as a Vocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_as_a_Vocation "Science as a Vocation") " and " [Politics as a Vocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_as_a_Vocation "Politics as a Vocation") ". After the war, Weber co-founded the [German Democratic Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Democratic_Party "German Democratic Party"), unsuccessfully ran for office, and advised the drafting of the [Weimar Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_Constitution "Weimar Constitution"). Meanwhile, he resumed teaching in [Vienna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Vienna "University of Vienna") and [Munich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LMU_Munich "LMU Munich"). He died of [pneumonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pneumonia "Pneumonia") in 1920 at the age of 56, possibly as a result of the post-war [Spanish flu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_flu "Spanish flu") pandemic. A book, *[Economy and Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_and_Society "Economy and Society")*, was left unfinished.

One of Weber's main intellectual concerns was in understanding the processes of [rationalisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalisation_\(sociology\) "Rationalisation (sociology)") and [disenchantment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disenchantment "Disenchantment"). He formulated a thesis arguing that such processes were associated with the rise of [capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalism "Capitalism") and modernity. Weber also argued that the [Protestant work ethic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestant_work_ethic "Protestant work ethic") influenced the creation of modern capitalism in *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*. It was followed by *[The Economic Ethics of the World Religions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Economic_Ethics_of_the_World_Religions "The Economic Ethics of the World Religions")*, where he examined the religions of [China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Religion_of_China "The Religion of China"), [India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Religion_of_India "The Religion of India"), and [ancient Judaism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Judaism_\(book\) "Ancient Judaism (book)"). In terms of government, Weber argued that [states](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)") were defined by their [monopoly on violence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monopoly_on_violence "Monopoly on violence") and categorised social authority into [three distinct forms](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripartite_classification_of_authority "Tripartite classification of authority"): [charismatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charismatic_authority "Charismatic authority"), [traditional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_authority "Traditional authority"), and [rational-legal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational-legal_authority "Rational-legal authority"). He was also a key proponent of methodological [antipositivism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipositivism "Antipositivism"), arguing for the study of [social action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_action "Social action") through [interpretive](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verstehen "Verstehen") rather than purely [empiricist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Empiricism "Empiricism") methods. Weber made a variety of other contributions to [economic sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_sociology "Economic sociology"), [political sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_sociology "Political sociology"), and the [sociology of religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology_of_religion "Sociology of religion").

After his death, the rise of Weberian scholarship was slowed by the [Weimar Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_Republic "Weimar Republic") 's political instability and the rise of [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany"). In the post-war era, organised scholarship began to appear, led by [Talcott Parsons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talcott_Parsons "Talcott Parsons"). Other American and British scholars were also involved in its development. Over the course of the twentieth century, Weber's reputation grew as translations of his works became widely available and scholars increasingly engaged with his life and ideas. As a result of these works, he began to be regarded as a founding father of sociology, alongside [Karl Marx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx "Karl Marx") and [Émile Durkheim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89mile_Durkheim "Émile Durkheim"), and one of the central figures in the development of the social sciences more generally.

## Biography

### Early life and education

Maximilian Carl Emil Weber was born on 21 April 1864 in [Erfurt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erfurt "Erfurt"), Province of Saxony, Kingdom of Prussia, and his family moved to [Charlottenburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charlottenburg "Charlottenburg") in 1869.[^2] He was the oldest of [Max Weber Sr.](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Weber_Sr. "Max Weber Sr.") and Helene Fallenstein's eight children.[^3] Over the course of his life, Weber Sr. held posts as a lawyer, civil servant, and parliamentarian for the [National Liberal Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National_Liberal_Party_\(Germany\) "National Liberal Party (Germany)") in the [Prussian Landtag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Landtag_of_Prussia "Landtag of Prussia") and [German Reichstag](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reichstag_\(German_Empire\) "Reichstag (German Empire)").[^4] This immersed his home in both politics and academia, as his [salon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salon_\(gathering\) "Salon (gathering)") welcomed scholars and public figures such as the philosopher [Wilhelm Dilthey](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Dilthey "Wilhelm Dilthey"), the jurist [Levin Goldschmidt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Levin_Goldschmidt "Levin Goldschmidt"), and the historian [Theodor Mommsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Mommsen "Theodor Mommsen"). The young Weber and his brother [Alfred](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Weber "Alfred Weber"), who also became a sociologist, spent their formative years in this intellectual atmosphere.[^5] Meanwhile, Fallenstein was partly descended from the French [Huguenot](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Huguenots "Huguenots") [Souchay family](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Souchay_family&action=edit&redlink=1 "Souchay family (page does not exist)"), which had obtained wealth through international commerce and the [textile industry](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Textile_industry "Textile industry").[^6] Over time, Weber was affected by the marital and personality tensions between his father, who enjoyed material pleasures while overlooking religious and [philanthropic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philanthropy "Philanthropy") causes, and his mother, a devout [Calvinist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calvinist "Calvinist") and philanthropist.[^7]

![A group photograph of Max Weber with his brothers Alfred and Karl](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/9d/Max_Weber_and_brothers_1879.jpg/250px-Max_Weber_and_brothers_1879.jpg)

Max Weber (left) and his brothers, Alfred (center) and Karl (right), in 1879

Weber entered the *Doebbelinsche Privatschule* in Charlottenburg in 1870, before attending the *[Kaiserin-Augusta-Gymnasium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kaiserin-Augusta-Gymnasium "Kaiserin-Augusta-Gymnasium")* between 1872 and 1882.[^8] While in class, bored and unimpressed with his teachers, Weber secretly read all forty of [Johann Friedrich Cotta](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Friedrich_Cotta_\(publisher\) "Johann Friedrich Cotta (publisher)") 's volumes of the writer [Johann Wolfgang von Goethe](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Johann_Wolfgang_von_Goethe "Johann Wolfgang von Goethe") 's works.[^9] Before graduating, he read many other classical works, including those by the philosopher [Immanuel Kant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant "Immanuel Kant").[^10] For Christmas in 1877, a thirteen-year-old Weber gifted his parents two historical essays, entitled "About the Course of German History, with Special Reference to the Positions of the Emperor and the Pope" and "About the Roman Imperial Period from Constantine to the Migration Period". Two years later, also during Christmastime, he wrote another historical essay, "Observations on the Ethnic Character, Development, and History of the Indo-European Nations". These three essays were non-derivative contributions to the [philosophy of history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_history "Philosophy of history") and resulted from Weber's reading of many sources.[^11]

In 1882, Weber enrolled in [Heidelberg University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heidelberg_University "Heidelberg University") as a law student, later studying at the [Royal Friedrich Wilhelm University of Berlin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Humboldt_University_of_Berlin "Humboldt University of Berlin") and the [University of Göttingen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_G%C3%B6ttingen "University of Göttingen").[^12] Weber's university years were dotted with periods of military service, the longest lasted between October 1883 and September 1884. During it, he attended classes that his uncle, the historian [Hermann Baumgarten](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermann_Baumgarten "Hermann Baumgarten"), taught at the [University of Strasbourg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Strasbourg "University of Strasbourg").[^13] Weber befriended Baumgarten, who influenced Weber's growing [liberalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberalism "Liberalism") and criticism of [Otto von Bismarck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Otto_von_Bismarck "Otto von Bismarck") 's domination of German politics.[^14] Weber was a member of the *[Burschenschaft Allemannia Heidelberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Burschenschaft_Allemannia_Heidelberg&action=edit&redlink=1 "Burschenschaft Allemannia Heidelberg (page does not exist)")*, a *[Studentenverbindung](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Studentenverbindung "Studentenverbindung")* (student association), and heavily drank beer and engaged in [academic fencing](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Academic_fencing "Academic fencing") during his first few university years.[^15] The latter resulted in him obtaining several [duelling scars](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Dueling_scar "Dueling scar") on the left side of his face.[^16] His mother was displeased by his behaviour and slapped him after he came home when his third semester ended in 1883. However, Weber matured, increasingly supported his mother in family arguments, and grew estranged from his father.[^17]

On 15 May 1886, Weber passed the [Referendar](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Referendary "Referendary") legal training examination.[^18] He practiced law and lectured simultaneously with his studies.[^19] Under the tutelage of Levin Goldschmidt and [Rudolf von Gneist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rudolf_von_Gneist "Rudolf von Gneist"), Weber wrote a legal history dissertation titled *Development of the Principle of Joint Liability and a Separate Fund of the General Partnership out of the Household Communities and Commercial Associations in Italian Cities* in 1889. It was part of a longer work, *[The History of Commercial Partnerships in the Middle Ages, Based on Southern European Documents](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_History_of_Commercial_Partnerships_in_the_Middle_Ages "The History of Commercial Partnerships in the Middle Ages")*, which he published later that year.[^20] In the same year, Weber began working on his [habilitation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Habilitation#Germany "Habilitation"), a post-doctoral thesis, with the statistician [August Meitzen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/August_Meitzen "August Meitzen") and completed it two years later. His habilitation, titled *[Roman Agrarian History and Its Significance for Public and Private Law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Roman_Agrarian_History_and_Its_Significance_for_Public_and_Private_Law "Roman Agrarian History and Its Significance for Public and Private Law")*, focused on Roman surveying and [Roman agrarian law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Agrarian_law "Agrarian law") 's relationship.[^21] Theodor Mommsen was present and debated with him during his [defence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Thesis#Germany_2 "Thesis"), later indicating that he wanted Weber to succeed him.[^22] Having thus become a *[Privatdozent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Privatdozent "Privatdozent")*, Weber joined the Royal Friedrich Wilhelm University of Berlin's faculty, lecturing, conducting research, and engaging in government consulting.[^23]

### Marriage, early work, and breakdown

![Max Weber, right, and Marianne, left, in 1894](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/62/Max_and_Marianne_Weber_1894.jpg/250px-Max_and_Marianne_Weber_1894.jpg)

Max Weber and his wife Marianne in 1894

From 1887 until her declining mental health caused him to end it five years later, Weber had a semi-engagement with Emmy Baumgarten, Hermann Baumgarten's daughter.[^24] Afterwards, he began a relationship with his second cousin [Marianne Schnitger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marianne_Weber "Marianne Weber") in 1893 and married her on 20 September of that year in [Oerlinghausen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oerlinghausen "Oerlinghausen").[^25] The marriage gave Weber financial independence, allowing him to leave his parents' household.[^26] They had no children.[^27] Marianne was a [feminist activist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Feminist_movement "Feminist movement") and an author in her own right.[^28] Academically, between completing his dissertation and habilitation, Weber became interested in contemporary [social policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_policy "Social policy") and joined the *[Verein für Socialpolitik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Verein_f%C3%BCr_Socialpolitik "Verein für Socialpolitik")* (Association for Social Policy) in 1888.[^29] The *Verein* was an organisation of reformist thinkers who were generally members of the [historical school of economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_school_of_economics "Historical school of economics").[^30] Weber also became politically involved, participating in the founding of the left-leaning [Evangelical Social Congress](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Evangelical_Social_Congress "Evangelical Social Congress") in 1890. It applied a [Protestant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestant "Protestant") perspective to the [social question](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_question "Social question") debate.[^31] In the same year, the *Verein* established a research program examining the *[Ostflucht](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostflucht "Ostflucht")*, which was the western migration of ethnically German [East Elbian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/East_Elbia "East Elbia") agricultural labourers and the corresponding influx of Polish farm workers. Weber was given control of the study and wrote part of the final report, generating attention and political controversy.[^32]

From 1893 to 1899, Weber was a [Pan-German League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pan-German_League "Pan-German League") (*Alldeutscher Verband*) member. They opposed the influx of Polish workers. How much he supported the [Germanisation of Poles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Germanisation_of_Poles_during_the_Partitions "Germanisation of Poles during the Partitions") and similar [German nationalist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_nationalist "German nationalist") policies is debated by scholars.[^33] Weber and his wife moved to [Freiburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Freiburg_im_Breisgau "Freiburg im Breisgau") in 1894, where he was appointed professor of economics at the [University of Freiburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Freiburg "University of Freiburg").[^34] His 1895 inaugural lecture, "The Nation State and Economic Policy", criticised Polish immigration and argued that the [Junkers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Junker_\(Prussia\) "Junker (Prussia)") encouraged it to serve their economic interests over those of Germany.[^35] It influenced the politician [Friedrich Naumann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Naumann "Friedrich Naumann") to create the [National-Social Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/National-Social_Association "National-Social Association"), which was a [Christian socialist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christian_socialist "Christian socialist") and German nationalist political organisation.[^36] Weber was pessimistic regarding its potential success, and it dissolved after winning a single Reichstag seat in the [1903 German federal election](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/1903_German_federal_election "1903 German federal election").[^37] In 1896, he was appointed to a [Heidelberg University](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heidelberg_University "Heidelberg University") economics and finance chair.[^38] There, Weber and his wife became central to the eponymous **Weber Circle**, which included [Georg Jellinek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Jellinek "Georg Jellinek"), [Ernst Troeltsch](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Troeltsch "Ernst Troeltsch"), and [Werner Sombart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Sombart "Werner Sombart"). Younger scholars, such as [György Lukács](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Luk%C3%A1cs "György Lukács") and [Robert Michels](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Robert_Michels "Robert Michels"), also joined it.[^39]

In June 1897, Weber had a severe quarrel with his father over his father's treatment of his mother.[^40] Weber Sr. died two months later during a trip to [Riga](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Riga "Riga"), leaving the argument unresolved.[^41] Afterwards, Weber became increasingly depressed, nervous, and [insomniac](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Insomnia "Insomnia"), making it difficult for him to fulfill his professorial duties.[^42] He sought a teaching exemption, which was granted in 1899. He stayed in the *Heilanstalt für Nervenkranke Konstanzer Hof* in 1898 and in a different [sanatorium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sanatorium "Sanatorium") in [Bad Urach](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bad_Urach "Bad Urach") in 1900.[^43] Weber also travelled to [Corsica](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Corsica "Corsica") and [Italy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Italy "Kingdom of Italy") between 1899 and 1903 to recuperate.[^44] He fully withdrew from teaching in 1903 and did not return to it until 1918.[^45] Weber thoroughly described his ordeal with mental illness in a personal [chronology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chronology "Chronology") that his widow later destroyed. She potentially feared that the [Nazis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Party "Nazi Party") would discredit his work if his experience with mental illness were widely known.[^46]

### Recovery and reintroduction to academia

![Max Weber in 1907, holding a hookah](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/Max_Weber_1907.jpg/250px-Max_Weber_1907.jpg)

Max Weber in 1907

After recovering from his illness, Weber became an associate editor of the *[Archiv für Sozialwissenschaft und Sozialpolitik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Archiv_f%C3%BCr_Sozialwissenschaft_und_Sozialpolitik "Archiv für Sozialwissenschaft und Sozialpolitik")* (*Archive for Social Science and Social Policy*) in 1904, alongside his colleagues [Edgar Jaffé](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edgar_Jaff%C3%A9 "Edgar Jaffé") and Werner Sombart.[^47] He made it one of the most prominent [social science](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science") journals.[^48] Weber published some of his most seminal works in it, including his book *[The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Protestant_Ethic_and_the_Spirit_of_Capitalism "The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism")*, which became his most famous work and influenced his later research on religion's impact on economic systems' development.[^49] Also in 1904, he participated in the Congress of Arts and Sciences that was part of the [Louisiana Purchase Exposition](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Louisiana_Purchase_Exposition "Louisiana Purchase Exposition") – the [World's fair](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World%27s_fair "World's fair") – in [St. Louis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/St._Louis "St. Louis"). He went alongside his wife, Werner Sombart, Ernst Troeltsch, and other German scholars.[^50] Taking advantage of it, the Webers travelled throughout the country for almost three months in a round trip that began and ended in New York City. Different communities were visited, including German immigrant towns and African American communities.[^51] He also visited some of his Fallenstein family relatives in [North Carolina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/North_Carolina "North Carolina").[^52] He learned more about America's social, economic, and theological conditions and their relationship with his thesis.[^53] Afterwards, Weber felt unable to resume teaching and remained a private scholar, helped by an inheritance in 1907.[^54]

Shortly after returning, Weber's attention shifted to the then-recent [Russian Revolution of 1905](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Revolution_of_1905 "Russian Revolution of 1905").[^55] He learned the [Russian language](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_language "Russian language") in a few months, subscribed to Russian newspapers, and discussed Russian political and social affairs with the [Heidelberg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heidelberg "Heidelberg") Russian *émigré* community.[^56] He was popular amongst them and twice considered travelling to [Russia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Empire "Russian Empire"), but his schedule prevented it.[^57] While he was sceptical of the revolution's ability to succeed, Weber supported the establishment of a Russian [liberal democracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Liberal_democracy "Liberal democracy").[^58] He wrote two essays on it that were published in the *Archiv*.[^59] Weber thought that the peasants' desire for land caused the revolution.[^60] He discussed the role of the *[obshchina](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Obshchina "Obshchina")*, rural peasant communities, in Russian political debates. According to Weber, they were difficult for liberal agrarian reformers to abolish due to their basis in [natural law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_law "Natural law") and the rising *[kulak](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kulak "Kulak")* class self-interestedly manipulating them.[^61] He generally interpreted the Russian Revolution as lacking a clear leader and the result of the peasants' emotional passions, not the Russian intellectuals' goals.[^62]

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/4/47/Jaffe_freiin_von_richthofen_else_1902.png/250px-Jaffe_freiin_von_richthofen_else_1902.png) ![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/7c/Mina_Tobler.jpg/250px-Mina_Tobler.jpg)

Else von Richthofen and Mina Tobler, Max Weber's mistresses

In 1909, having become increasingly dissatisfied with the political conservatism and perceived lack of methodological discipline of the *Verein*, he co-founded the [German Sociological Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Sociological_Association "German Sociological Association") (*Deutsche Gesellschaft für Soziologie*) and was its first treasurer.[^63] Weber associated it with the *Verein* and viewed them as not having been competitors.[^64] He unsuccessfully tried to steer the association's direction.[^65] As part of that, Weber tried to make the *Archiv* its official journal.[^66] He resigned from his position as treasurer in 1912.[^67] His support for [value-freedom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value-freedom "Value-freedom") in the social sciences caused that, as it was a controversial position in the association.[^68] Also in 1909, Weber and his wife Marianne traveled to Venice with [Else von Richthofen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Else_von_Richthofen "Else von Richthofen"), a former student of his and her longtime friend. The couple also befriended pianist [Mina Tobler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mina_Tobler "Mina Tobler"). After failing to court Richthofen, Weber began an affair with Tobler in 1911.[^69] Multiple scholars and Weber's nephew [Eduard Baumgarten](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Eduard_Baumgarten&action=edit&redlink=1 "Eduard Baumgarten (page does not exist)") have attributed Weber's "Intermediate Reflection" on religion, salvation, and the erotic, to his feelings for Richthofen and Tobler.[^70]

Later, during the spring of 1913, Weber holidayed in the [Monte Verità](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monte_Verit%C3%A0 "Monte Verità") community in [Ascona](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ascona "Ascona"), [Switzerland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Switzerland "Switzerland").[^71] While holidaying, he advised Frieda Gross in her custody battle for her children. He opposed [Erich Mühsam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_M%C3%BChsam "Erich Mühsam") 's involvement because of Mühsam's [anarchism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anarchism "Anarchism"). Weber argued that the case needed to be dealt with by [bourgeois](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bourgeois "Bourgeois") reformers who were not "derailed".[^72] A year later, also in spring, he again holidayed in Ascona.[^73] The community contained several different then-contemporaneous radical political and lifestyle reform movements, including [naturism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Naturism "Naturism"), [free love](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Free_love "Free love"), and [Western esotericism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_esotericism "Western esotericism"), among others. Weber was critical of the Ascona anarchist and erotic movements, as he viewed their fusion as having been politically absurd.[^74]

### First World War and political involvement

After the [First World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/First_World_War "First World War") began in 1914, Weber volunteered for service and was appointed as a [reserve officer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Military_reserve_force "Military reserve force") in charge of organising the Heidelberg army hospitals, a role he fulfilled until the end of 1915.[^75] Over the course of it, his views on the war and the [German Empire](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Empire "German Empire") 's expansion changed.[^76] Early on, he strongly supported the [German war effort](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_Germany_during_World_War_I "History of Germany during World War I"). However, Weber later became one of the most prominent critics of both [German expansionism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lebensraum#First_World_War_nationalist_premise "Lebensraum") and the [Kaiser's war policies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_II#World_War_I "Wilhelm II").[^77] He publicly criticised [Germany's potential annexation of Belgium](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Septemberprogramm "Septemberprogramm") and [unrestricted submarine warfare](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Unrestricted_submarine_warfare "Unrestricted submarine warfare"), later supporting calls for constitutional reform, [democratisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratisation "Democratisation"), and [universal suffrage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Universal_suffrage "Universal suffrage").[^78] The death of his previously derided younger brother Karl, an architect, while fighting near [Brest-Litovsk](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brest-Litovsk "Brest-Litovsk") in 1915 made him relate to him.[^79]

![Max Weber, facing right, lecturing with Ernst Toller in the center of the background](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/f/fd/Max_Weber_in_Lauenstein%2C_1917.png/250px-Max_Weber_in_Lauenstein%2C_1917.png)

Max Weber (facing right) with Ernst Toller (facing camera) during the Lauenstein Conferences in 1917

He and his wife participated in the 1917 Lauenstein Conferences at [Lauenstein Castle](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Lauenstein_Castle&action=edit&redlink=1 "Lauenstein Castle (page does not exist)") in [Bavaria](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kingdom_of_Bavaria "Kingdom of Bavaria").[^80] These conferences were planned by the publisher [Eugen Diederichs](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eugen_Diederichs "Eugen Diederichs") and brought together intellectuals, including [Theodor Heuss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_Heuss "Theodor Heuss"), [Ernst Toller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ernst_Toller "Ernst Toller"), and Werner Sombart.[^81] Weber's presence elevated his profile in Germany and dispelled some of the event's [romantic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Romanticism "Romanticism") atmosphere. He was involved in the planning for the second one, as Diederichs thought that the conferences needed an oppositional figure. Weber argued against the former theologian [Max Maurenbrecher](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Maurenbrecher "Max Maurenbrecher") 's political romanticism and also opposed what he saw as the youth groups and nationalists' excessive rhetoric, instead supporting German democratisation.[^82] For him and the younger participants, the conferences' romanticism could not determine Germany's future.[^83] In November, shortly after the second conference, Weber was invited by the Free Student Youth – a student organisation – to lecture in Munich, resulting in " [Science as a Vocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_as_a_Vocation "Science as a Vocation") ".[^84] In it, he argued that an inner calling and specialisation were necessary for one to become a scholar.[^85] Weber also began a [sadomasochistic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sadomasochistic "Sadomasochistic") affair with Else von Richthofen the next year.[^86] Meanwhile, she was simultaneously conducting an affair with his brother, [Alfred](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Weber "Alfred Weber").[^87] Max Weber's affairs with Richtofen and Mina Tobler lasted until his death in 1920.[^88]

After the war, Weber unsuccessfully ran for a [Weimar National Assembly](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_National_Assembly "Weimar National Assembly") seat in January 1919 for the liberal [German Democratic Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Democratic_Party "German Democratic Party"), which he had co-founded.[^89] He also advised the National Assembly's drafting of the [Weimar Constitution](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_Constitution "Weimar Constitution").[^90] While campaigning, Weber critiqued the left and criticised the leftist [Spartacus League](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spartacus_League "Spartacus League") 's leaders, [Karl Liebknecht](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Liebknecht "Karl Liebknecht") and [Rosa Luxemburg](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rosa_Luxemburg "Rosa Luxemburg").[^91] He thought that the [German Revolution of 1918–1919](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Revolution_of_1918%E2%80%931919 "German Revolution of 1918–1919") prevented Germany from contesting [Poland](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_Polish_Republic "Second Polish Republic") 's claims on its eastern territories.[^92] His opposition to the revolution may have prevented [Friedrich Ebert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Ebert "Friedrich Ebert"), the new [Social Democratic Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_Democratic_Party_of_Germany "Social Democratic Party of Germany") [president of Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/President_of_Germany_\(1919%E2%80%931945\) "President of Germany (1919–1945)"), from appointing him as a minister or ambassador.[^93] Weber also criticized the [Treaty of Versailles](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Treaty_of_Versailles "Treaty of Versailles"), which he believed unjustly [assigned war guilt to Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Article_231_of_the_Treaty_of_Versailles "Article 231 of the Treaty of Versailles").[^94] Instead, he believed that many countries were guilty, not just Germany.[^95] Weber argued that Russia was the only [great power](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Great_power "Great power") that desired the war.[^96] Overall, Weber's political efforts were unsuccessful, other than supporting a democratically elected and strong presidency.[^97] On 28 January 1919, after his electoral defeat, Weber delivered a lecture titled " [Politics as a Vocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Politics_as_a_Vocation "Politics as a Vocation") ", which discussed politics.[^98] It was prompted by the early [Weimar Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_Republic "Weimar Republic") 's political turmoil and was requested by the Free Student Youth.[^99] Shortly before joining the Versailles delegation on 13 May 1919, Weber used his connections with the [German National People's Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_National_People%27s_Party "German National People's Party") 's deputies to meet [Erich Ludendorff](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Erich_Ludendorff "Erich Ludendorff") and spent several hours unsuccessfully convincing him to surrender himself to the [Allies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Allies_of_World_War_I "Allies of World War I").[^100]

### Last years

Partially because he and his family lost money investing in [war bonds](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/War_bond "War bond"), Weber resumed teaching, first at the [University of Vienna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/University_of_Vienna "University of Vienna") in 1918, then at [LMU Munich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LMU_Munich "LMU Munich") in 1919.[^101] In [Vienna](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vienna "Vienna"), Weber filled a vacant political economy chair that he had been considered for since October 1917.[^102] While there, he gave a lecture titled "Socialism" on [the eponymous subject](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Socialism "Socialism") to a group of [Austro-Hungarian Army](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austro-Hungarian_Army "Austro-Hungarian Army") officers.[^103] He also had a heated debate with [Joseph Schumpeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Schumpeter "Joseph Schumpeter") at the [Café Landtmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caf%C3%A9_Landtmann "Café Landtmann") on the usability of the [Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Russian_Soviet_Federative_Socialist_Republic "Russian Soviet Federative Socialist Republic") as a case study for [communism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Communism "Communism").[^104] Later, in [Munich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Munich "Munich"), Weber was appointed to [Lujo Brentano](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Lujo_Brentano "Lujo Brentano") 's social science, economic history, and political economy chair. He accepted it to be closer to his mistress, Else von Richthofen.[^105] Responding to student requests, he gave a lecture series on [economic history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_history "Economic history"). The student transcriptions were later edited and published as the *[General Economic History](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/General_Economic_History "General Economic History")* by [Siegmund Hellmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Siegmund_Hellmann&action=edit&redlink=1 "Siegmund Hellmann (page does not exist)") and [Melchior Palyi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melchior_Palyi "Melchior Palyi") in 1923.[^106] In terms of politics, he opposed the pardoning of the [Bavarian Minister-President](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/List_of_minister-presidents_of_Bavaria "List of minister-presidents of Bavaria") [Kurt Eisner](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kurt_Eisner "Kurt Eisner") 's murderer, [Anton Graf von Arco auf Valley](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anton_Graf_von_Arco_auf_Valley "Anton Graf von Arco auf Valley"). In response, right-wing students disrupted his classes and protested outside his home.[^107] In early 1920, Weber discussed [Oswald Spengler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Spengler "Oswald Spengler") 's *[The Decline of the West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Decline_of_the_West "The Decline of the West")* in a seminar, provoking students – who personally knew Spengler – to suggest that he debate Spengler alongside other scholars. They debated for two days in the [Munich town hall](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/New_Town_Hall_\(Munich\) "New Town Hall (Munich)"), but the students felt that the question of how to resolve Germany's post-war issues remained unanswered.[^108]

![A photograph of Max Weber's grave](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/63/Max_weber.JPG/250px-Max_weber.JPG)

Max Weber's grave in Heidelberg

Lili Schäfer, one of Weber's sisters, unexpectedly committed suicide on 7 April 1920 after the pedagogue [Paul Geheeb](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Paul_Geheeb "Paul Geheeb") ended his affair with her.[^109] As a result of her [depression](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Depression_\(mood\) "Depression (mood)"), Weber had been taking care of her after her husband's death on 26 August 1914. Since he had also previously experienced suicidal feelings, he respected it, as he thought that her suicide was justifiable and that suicide in general could be honourable.[^110] The Webers took in Lili's four children and planned to raise them. Weber was uncomfortable with his newfound role as a father figure, but thought that it fulfilled Marianne as a woman.[^111] She formally adopted the children in 1927.[^112] Weber wanted her to stay with them in Heidelberg or move closer to Geheeb's *[Odenwaldschule](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Odenwaldschule "Odenwaldschule")* (Odenwald School) so that he could be alone in Munich with his mistress, [Else von Richthofen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Else_von_Richthofen "Else von Richthofen"). He left the decision to Marianne, but she said that only he could make the decision to leave for himself.[^113] While this was occurring, Weber began to believe that his own life had reached its end.[^114]

On 4 June 1920, Weber's students were informed that he had a cold and needed to cancel classes. By 14 June 1920, the cold had turned into [influenza](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Influenza "Influenza") and he died of [pneumonia](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pneumonia "Pneumonia") in Munich.[^115] He had likely contracted the [Spanish flu](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spanish_flu "Spanish flu") during the post-war pandemic and been subjected to insufficient medical care. Else von Richthofen, who was present by his deathbed alongside his wife, thought that he could have survived his illness if he had been given better treatment.[^116] His body was cremated in the Munich *[Ostfriedhof](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ostfriedhof_\(Munich\) "Ostfriedhof (Munich)")* after a secular ceremony, and his cinerary urn was later buried in the Heidelberg *[Bergfriedhof](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Bergfriedhof&action=edit&redlink=1 "Bergfriedhof (page does not exist)")* in 1921. The funeral service was attended by his students, including [Eduard Baumgarten](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Eduard_Baumgarten&action=edit&redlink=1 "Eduard Baumgarten (page does not exist)") and [Karl Loewenstein](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Loewenstein "Karl Loewenstein"), and fellow scholars, such as Lujo Brentano.[^117] Weber had not finished writing *[Economy and Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_and_Society "Economy and Society")*, his sociological theory *magnum opus*. His widow, Marianne, helped prepare it for its publication between 1921 and 1922.[^118] She later published a Weber biography in 1926 which became one of the central historical accounts of his life.[^119]

## Methodology

[Social action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_action "Social action") was the central focus of Weber's sociology.[^120] He also considered it an important part of the field's scientific nature.[^121] He divided social action into the four categories of [affectional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Affectional_action "Affectional action"), [traditional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_action "Traditional action"), [instrumental, and value-rational action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Instrumental_and_value_rationality "Instrumental and value rationality").[^122] Affectional actions were caused by an actor's emotions.[^123] Traditional actions were based on tradition.[^124] Instrumental actions were calculated attempts to achieve goals.[^125] Value-rational actions were attempts to enact one's values.[^126] His methodology was different from those of [Émile Durkheim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89mile_Durkheim "Émile Durkheim") and [Karl Marx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx "Karl Marx") in that he primarily focused on individuals and culture.[^127] Whereas Durkheim focused on society, Weber concentrated on [individuals](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Individualism "Individualism") and their actions. Meanwhile, compared to Marx's support for the material world's primacy over the world of ideas, Weber valued ideas as motivating individuals' actions.[^128] His perspective on [structure and action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structure_and_agency "Structure and agency") and [macrostructure](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Macrostructure_\(sociology\) "Macrostructure (sociology)") differed from theirs in that he was open to the idea that [social phenomena](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_phenomena "Social phenomena") could have multiple causes and placed importance on [social actors](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_actor "Social actor") ' interpretations of their actions.[^127]

### Verstehen

> The result of what has been said so far is that an "objective" treatment of cultural occurrences, in the sense that the ideal aim of scientific work would be to reduce the empirical \[reality\] to "laws", is absurd. *Not* because – as it has often been claimed – the course of cultural processes or, say, processes in the human mind would, "objectively" speaking, be less law-like, but for the following two reasons: (1) knowledge of social laws does not constitute knowledge of social reality, but is only one of the various tools that our intellect needs for that \[latter\] purpose; (2) knowledge of *cultural* occurrences is only conceivable if it takes as its point of departure the *significance* that the reality of life, with its always individual character, has for us in certain *particular* respects. No law can reveal to us in *what* sense and in *what* respects this will be the case, as that is determined by those *value ideas* in the light of which we look at "culture" in each individual case.

— Max Weber in "The 'Objectivity' of Knowledge in Social Science and Social Policy", 1904.[^129]

In terms of methodology, Weber was primarily concerned with [objectivity and subjectivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Objectivity_and_subjectivity "Objectivity and subjectivity"), distinguishing social action from [social behavior](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_behavior "Social behavior"), and noting that social action must be understood through individuals' subjective relationships.[^130] According to him, the study of social action through interpretive means or *verstehen* (to understand) needed to be based upon understanding the [subjective meaning](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Meaning-making "Meaning-making") and purpose that individuals attached to their actions.[^131] Determining an individual's interpretation of their actions required either empathically or rationally derived evidence.[^132] Weber noted that subjectivity's importance in the social sciences made the creation of fool-proof, universal laws harder than in the [natural sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Natural_science "Natural science") and that the amount of objective knowledge that social sciences were able to create was limited.[^133] Overall, he supported objective science as a goal worth striving for but noted that it was ultimately unreachable.[^134]

Weber's methodology was developed in the context of wider social scientific methodological debates.[^135] The first of which was the *[Methodenstreit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methodenstreit "Methodenstreit")* (method dispute).[^136] In it, he was close to [historicism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historicism "Historicism"), as he thought that social actions were heavily tied to historical contexts.[^137] He also emphasised the use of [comparative historical analysis](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Comparative_history "Comparative history") and was more interested in explaining how an outcome was the result of various historical processes than in predicting those processes' future outcomes.[^138] The second debate that shaped Weber's perspective on methodology was the *[Werturteilsstreit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werturteilsstreit "Werturteilsstreit")* (value-judgement dispute).[^139] This debate was held between 1909 and 1914 on [value-judgements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value-judgement "Value-judgement") in the social sciences. It originated with a *Verein für Socialpolitik* debate between the supporters and opponents of the idea that ethics was an important consideration in the field of economics.[^140] Weber's position was that the social sciences should strive to be [value-free](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Value-free "Value-free").[^141] In his view, [science had no part in choosing values](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fact%E2%80%93value_distinction "Fact–value distinction"). With regards to economics, he argued that [productivity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Productivity "Productivity") was not a useful scientific concept, as it could impede the proper evaluation of economic phenomena.[^142]

### Methodological individualism

The principle of methodological individualism, which held that social scientists should seek to understand collectivities solely as the result of individual people's actions, originated with Weber.[^143] The term "methodological individualism" was coined in 1908 by the Austrian-American economist [Joseph Schumpeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Schumpeter "Joseph Schumpeter") as a name for Weber's views on explaining social phenomena.[^144] While his research interests placed a strong emphasis on interpreting [economic history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_history "Economic history"), Weber's support of methodological individualism broke with the [historical school of economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_school_of_economics "Historical school of economics") and aligned with the [Austrian school of economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austrian_school_of_economics "Austrian school of economics") ' founder, [Carl Menger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Menger "Carl Menger"), in the *Methodenstreit*.[^145] In the first chapter of *[Economy and Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_and_Society "Economy and Society")*, Weber argued that only individuals "can be treated as agents in a course of subjectively understandable action".[^143] Despite the term's usage of "individualism", he did not interpret the individual as being the true source for sociological explanations. Instead, while only individuals could engage in intentional action, they were not necessarily separate from the collective group.[^146] Weber thought that methodological individualism had a close proximity to *verstehende* (interpretive) sociology, as actions could be interpreted subjectively. Similarly, it was also related to ideal types in that it involved discussions of abstract and rational models of human behaviour.[^147]

### Ideal type

The ideal type was a central concept in Weber's methodology.[^148] He argued that they were indispensable for the social sciences.[^149] Due to taking of meaning into account, they were unique to the social sciences.[^150] An ideal type was a schematic that represented a social action and considered the role of meaning in it.[^151] It was an exaggeration of an empirical situation through its assumption that the involved individuals were rational, had complete situational knowledge, were completely aware of the situation, were completely aware of their actions, and made no errors.[^152] This was then contrasted with empirical reality, allowing the researcher to better understand it.[^153] However, ideal types were not direct representations of reality and Weber warned against interpreting them as such.[^154] The ideal types' three functions were to formulate terminology, classifications, and hypotheses.[^155] The latter task was the most important one.[^156] He placed no limits on what ideal types could be used to analyse.[^157] Since he thought that rational methodology and science were synonymous, ideal types were constructed rationally.[^158] The term "ideal type" was derived from [Georg Jellinek](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Georg_Jellinek "Georg Jellinek").[^159] Weber outlined it in " [The 'Objectivity' of Knowledge in Social Science and Social Policy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_%22Objectivity%22_of_Knowledge_in_Social_Science_and_Social_Policy "The \"Objectivity\" of Knowledge in Social Science and Social Policy") " and the first chapter of *Economy and Society*.[^160]

### Value-freedom

Weber thought that [social scientists needed to avoid making value-judgements](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Fact%E2%80%93value_distinction "Fact–value distinction"). Instead, social scientific research needed to be value-free.[^161] This would give scientists objectivity but must combine with an acknowledgement that their research connected with values in different ways.[^162] As part of his support for value-freedom, Weber opposed both instructors and students promoting their political views in the classroom.[^163] He first articulated it in his scientific philosophy writings, including "The 'Objectivity' of Knowledge in Social Science and Social Policy" and "Science as a Vocation".[^164] Weber was influenced by [Heinrich Rickert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Rickert "Heinrich Rickert") 's concept of value-relevance.[^165] Rickert used it to relate historical objects to values while maintaining objectivity through explicitly defined conceptual distinctions.[^166] However, Weber disagreed with the idea that a scholar could maintain objectivity while ascribing to a hierarchy of values in the way that Rickert did, however.[^167] His argument regarding value-freedom was connected to his involvement in the *Werturteilsstreit*.[^140] During it, he argued that the social sciences needed to be value-free.[^168] Meanwhile, he unsuccessfully tried to turn the [German Sociological Association](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_Sociological_Association "German Sociological Association") into a value-free organisation.[^169]

## Theories

### Rationalisation

Rationalisation was a central theme in Weber's scholarship.[^170] This theme was situated in the larger context of the relationship between [psychological](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychological "Psychological") motivations, cultural values, cultural beliefs, and societal structure.[^171] Weber understood rationalisation as having resulted in increasing knowledge, growing impersonality, and the enhanced control of social and material life.[^172] He was ambivalent towards it. Weber admitted that it was responsible for many advances, particularly freeing humans from traditional, restrictive, and illogical social guidelines. However, he also criticised it for dehumanising individuals and curtailing their freedom, trapping them in the rational and bureaucratic [iron cage](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Iron_cage "Iron cage").[^173] His studies of it began with *[The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Protestant_Ethic_and_the_Spirit_of_Capitalism "The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism")*.[^174] In it, he argued that [Protestantism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestantism "Protestantism") 's – particularly [Calvinism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calvinism "Calvinism") 's – redefinition of work and [piety](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Piety "Piety") 's connection caused a shift towards rational attempts to achieve economic gain.[^175] In Protestantism, piety was expressed through one's secular vocation.[^176] The religious principles that influenced [capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalism "Capitalism") 's creation became unnecessary and it became able to propagate itself without them.[^177]

> What Weber depicted was not only the secularisation of Western *culture*, but also and especially the development of modern *societies* from the viewpoint of rationalisation. The new structures of society were marked by the differentiation of the two functionally intermeshing systems that had taken shape around the organisational cores of the capitalist enterprise and the bureaucratic state apparatus. Weber understood this process as the institutionalisation of purposive-rational economic and administrative action. To the degree that everyday life was affected by this cultural and societal rationalisation, traditional forms of life – which in the early modern period were differentiated primarily according to one's trade – were dissolved.

—  [Jürgen Habermas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas "Jürgen Habermas") in *The Philosophical Discourse of Modernity: Twelve Lectures*, 1990.[^178]

Weber continued his investigation into rationalisation in later works, notably in his studies on [bureaucracy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bureaucracy "Bureaucracy") and the classification of legitimate [authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Authority "Authority") into three [ideal types](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_types "Ideal types") – [rational-legal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational-legal_authority "Rational-legal authority"), [traditional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_authority "Traditional authority"), and [charismatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charismatic_authority "Charismatic authority") – of which rational-legal was the dominant one in modernity.[^179] In these works, Weber described what he saw as society's movement towards rationalisation.[^180] Bureaucratic states justified themselves through their own rationality and were supported by expert knowledge which made them rational.[^181] Rationalisation could also be seen in the economy, with the development of a highly rational capitalism. Capitalism's rationality related to its basis in [economic calculation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_calculation "Economic calculation"), particularly [double-entry bookkeeping](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Double-entry_bookkeeping "Double-entry bookkeeping"), which separated it from alternative forms of economic organisation.[^182] State bureaucracy and capitalism served as the twin pillars of the developing rational society. These changes eliminated the preexisting traditions that relied on the trades.[^178] Weber also saw rationalisation as one of the main factors that set the West apart from the rest of the world.[^183] Furthermore, *The Rational and Social Foundations of Music* was his application of rationalisation to music.[^184] It was influenced by his affair with the pianist [Mina Tobler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mina_Tobler "Mina Tobler") and a sense that [Western music](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_culture#Music "Western culture") was the only type that had become harmonic, while other cultures' music was more intense and focused on hearing.[^185] Weber argued that music was becoming increasingly rational. In his view, that resulted from new developments in musical instrument construction and simultaneous socio-economic shifts of the different instruments' players.[^186]

### Disenchantment

The process of disenchantment progressively removed magic as a central part of people's interpretation of the world.[^187] Older explanations of events relied on the belief in supernatural interference in the material world.[^188] According to Weber, religious activity began with material world actions that were given magical meanings and associated with vague [spirits](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Spirit_\(supernatural_entity\) "Spirit (supernatural entity)").[^189] Over time, this became increasingly systemised and the spirits became gods, resulting in polytheism and [organised religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Organised_religion "Organised religion").[^189] Increasing rationality caused Western monotheism to develop, and groups focused on specific gods for political and economic purposes, creating a universal religion.[^190] Protestantism encouraged an increased pursuit of rationality that led to its own devaluation.[^191] It was replaced by modern science, which was considered a valid alternative to religious belief.[^192] However, modern science also ceased to be a source for universal values, as it could not create values.[^193] In its place was a collection of different value systems that could not adequately replace it.[^194] This mirrored the previous state of Western polytheism, but differed from it in that its gods lacked their ancient predecessors' mystical qualities.[^195]

### The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism

> The development of the concept of the calling quickly gave to the modern entrepreneur a fabulously clear conscience – and also industrious workers; he gave to his employees as the wages of their ascetic devotion to the calling and of co-operation in his ruthless exploitation of them through capitalism the prospect of eternal salvation.

— Max Weber in *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*, 1905.[^176]

*The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* is Weber's most famous work.[^196] It was his first work on how religions affected economic systems' development.[^197] In the book, he put forward the thesis that the [Protestant work ethic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Protestant_work_ethic "Protestant work ethic"), which was derived from the theological ideas of the [Reformation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reformation "Reformation"), influenced capitalism's development.[^198] Weber looked for elective affinities between the Protestant work ethic and capitalism.[^199] He may have derived "elective affinity" from [one of Johann Wolfgang von Goethe's works](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elective_Affinities "Elective Affinities").[^200] Weber argued that the Puritans' [religious calling](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Vocation "Vocation") to work caused them to systematically obtain wealth.[^201] They wished to prove that they were [predestined](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predestined "Predestined") to go to Heaven.[^202] Weber used [Benjamin Franklin](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Benjamin_Franklin "Benjamin Franklin") 's personal ethic, as described in his " [Advice to a Young Tradesman](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Advice_to_a_Young_Tradesman&action=edit&redlink=1 "Advice to a Young Tradesman (page does not exist)") ", as an example of the Protestant sects' economic ethic.[^203] Concepts that later became central to his scholarship, including [rationalisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalization_\(sociology\) "Rationalization (sociology)") and the [ideal type](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ideal_type "Ideal type"), appeared in the text.[^174]

According to Weber, certain types of Protestantism – notably [Calvinism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Calvinism "Calvinism") – were supportive of the rational pursuit of economic gain and the worldly activities that were dedicated to it, seeing those activities as having been endowed with moral and spiritual significance.[^204] The spirit of capitalism was found in the desire to work hard in a way that pleased the worker and signified their worth and originally had a basis in theology.[^205] In particular, the Protestant work ethic motivated the believers to work hard, be successful in business, and reinvest their profits in further development rather than frivolous pleasures.[^206] Weber thought that self-restraint, hard work, and a belief that wealth could be a sign of [salvation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Salvation "Salvation") were representative of [ascetic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ascetic "Ascetic") Protestantism.[^207] Ascetic Protestants practiced [inner-worldly asceticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner-worldly_asceticism "Inner-worldly asceticism") and sought to change the world to better reflect their beliefs.[^208] The notion of a religious calling, when combined with [predestination](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Predestination "Predestination"), meant that each individual had to take action to prove their salvation to themselves.[^209] However, the success that these religious principles created ultimately removed them as an influence on modern capitalism through their creation of a worldly perspective. As a result, rationalisation entrapped that system's inheritors in a socio-economic iron cage.[^177]

### The Economic Ethics of the World Religions

Weber's work in the field of [sociology of religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology_of_religion "Sociology of religion") began with the book *[The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Protestant_Ethic_and_the_Spirit_of_Capitalism "The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism")*.[^210] It continued with *The Economic Ethics of the World Religions* book series, which contained *[The Religion of China](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Religion_of_China "The Religion of China")*, *[The Religion of India](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Religion_of_India "The Religion of India")*, and *[Ancient Judaism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ancient_Judaism_\(book\) "Ancient Judaism (book)")*.[^211] Intended later studies of [Christianity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Christianity "Christianity"), [Talmudic Judaism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talmudic_Judaism "Talmudic Judaism"), and [Islam](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Islam "Islam") were left unwritten.[^212] The books' three main themes were: religious ideas' effect on economic activities, the relationship between [social stratification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_stratification "Social stratification") and religious ideas, and the distinguishable characteristics of [Western civilisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_culture "Western culture").[^213] Weber's goal was to explain the [Western world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_world "Western world") and the [Eastern world](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_world "Eastern world") 's different developmental paths and the distinctive elements of Western civilisation.[^214] Weber also proposed a [socio-evolutionary](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociocultural_evolution "Sociocultural evolution") model of religious change where societies moved from magic to [ethical monotheism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethical_monotheism "Ethical monotheism"), with the intermediatory steps of [polytheism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Polytheism "Polytheism"), [pantheism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Pantheism "Pantheism"), and [monotheism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monotheism "Monotheism").[^215] According to him, this resulted from growing economic stability, allowing for [professionalisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Professionalisation "Professionalisation") and the evolution of an increasingly sophisticated priesthood.[^216] As societies grew more complex and encompassed different groups, a hierarchy of gods developed. Meanwhile, as their power became more centralised, the concept of a universal God became more popular and desirable.[^217]

#### The Religion of China

In *The Religion of China: Confucianism and Taoism*, Weber focused on the aspects of Chinese society that differed from those of Western Europe, especially ones that contrasted with [Puritanism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Puritans#Beliefs "Puritans"). As part of that, he questioned why capitalism had not developed in China.[^218] He focused on the issues of Chinese urban development, Chinese [patrimonialism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Patrimonialism "Patrimonialism") and officialdom and [Chinese religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religion_in_China "Religion in China") and [philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Chinese_philosophy "Chinese philosophy") – primarily [Confucianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Confucianism "Confucianism") and [Taoism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Taoism "Taoism") – as the areas in which Chinese significantly differed from European development.[^219] According to Weber, Confucianism and Puritanism were superficially similar, but were actually very different from one another.[^220] Instead, they were mutually exclusive types of [rational thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalization_\(sociology\) "Rationalization (sociology)"), each attempting to prescribe a way of life based on religious dogma.[^221] Notably, they both valued self-control and restraint and did not oppose wealth accumulation. However, both of those qualities were simply means to different final goals.[^222] Confucianism's goal was "a cultured status position", while Puritanism's goal was to make individuals "tools of God". According to Weber, the Puritans sought rational control of the world and rejected its irrationality while Confucians sought rational acceptance of that state of affairs.[^223] Therefore, he stated that the difference in social attitudes and mentality, shaped by the respective dominant religions, contributed to the development of capitalism in the West and the absence of it in China.[^224]

#### The Religion of India

In *The Religion of India: The Sociology of Hinduism and Buddhism*, Weber dealt with Indian society's structure and what he interpreted as the [orthodox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Orthodoxy "Orthodoxy") doctrines of [Hinduism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hinduism "Hinduism") and the [heterodox](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heterodoxy "Heterodoxy") doctrines of [Buddhism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Buddhism "Buddhism") and [Jainism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jainism "Jainism").[^225] In Weber's view, Hinduism in India, like Confucianism in China, was a barrier for capitalism.[^226] The [Indian caste system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Caste_system_in_India "Caste system in India"), which developed in post-Classical India and served as the source for legitimate social interactions, served as a key part of that.[^227] Both Hinduism and the [Brahmins](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Brahmin "Brahmin") ' high status upheld the caste system.[^228] The Brahmins used their monopoly on education and theological authority to maintain their position.[^229] Meanwhile, Hinduism created a psychological justification for it through the cycle of [reincarnation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reincarnation "Reincarnation").[^230] In it, a person's caste position was determined by one's past life actions.[^231] As a result, [soul](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Soul "Soul") advancement and obeying the predetermined order were more important than seeking material world advancement, including economic advancement.[^232]

Weber ended his research of Indian society and religion by using insights from his previous work on China to discuss the similarities of the Asian belief systems.[^233] He noted that these religions' believers used otherworldly [mystical experiences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religious_experience "Religious experience") to interpret life's meaning.[^234] The social world was fundamentally divided between the educated elite who followed the guidance of a [prophet](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prophet "Prophet") or wise man and the uneducated masses whose beliefs were centered on magic. In Asia, there were no [messianic prophecies](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Messianic_prophecies "Messianic prophecies") to give both educated and uneducated followers meaning in their regular lives.[^235] Weber juxtaposed such Messianic prophecies, notably from the [Near East](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Near_East "Near East"), with the exemplary prophecies found in mainland Asia that focused more on reaching to the educated elites and enlightening them on the proper ways to live one's life, usually with little emphasis on hard work and the material world.[^236] It was those differences that prevented Western countries from following the paths of the earlier Chinese and Indian civilisations. His next work, *Ancient Judaism*, was an attempt to prove this theory.[^237]

#### Ancient Judaism

In *Ancient Judaism*, Weber attempted to explain the factors that resulted in the early differences between [Eastern](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Eastern_world "Eastern world") and [Western](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Western_world "Western world") [religiosity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Religiosity "Religiosity").[^238] He contrasted the [inner-worldly asceticism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Inner-worldly_asceticism "Inner-worldly asceticism") developed by Western Christianity with the mystical contemplation that developed in India.[^239] Weber noted that some aspects of Christianity sought to conquer and change the world, rather than withdraw from its imperfections.[^240] This fundamental characteristic of Christianity originally stemmed from ancient Jewish [prophecy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Prophecy "Prophecy").[^241] Weber classified the Jewish people as having been a pariah people, meaning that they were separated from the society that contained them.[^242] He examined the ancient Jewish people's origins and social structures.[^243] In his view, the [Israelites](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Israelites "Israelites") maintained order through a [covenant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Covenant_\(biblical\) "Covenant (biblical)") with the war god [Yahweh](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Yahweh "Yahweh") and the practice of warrior asceticism.[^244] Under [Solomon](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Solomon "Solomon"), that changed into a more organised and law-based society than the old confederation was.[^245] Religiously, the priests replaced the previous charismatic religious leaders. Weber thought that [Elijah](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Elijah "Elijah") was the first prophet to have risen from the shepherds. Elijah promulgated political prophecies and opposed the [monarchy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kings_of_Israel_and_Judah "Kings of Israel and Judah").[^246]

### Theodicy

Weber used the concept of theodicy in his interpretation of theology and religion throughout his corpus.[^247] This involved both his scholarly and personal interests in the subject. It was central to his conception of humanity, which he interpreted as being connected with finding meaning.[^248] Weber's personal perspective on religion was that he was "unmusical religiously", but "neither antireligious *nor irreligious* ".[^249] Theodicy was a popular subject of study amongst German scholars who sought to determine how a world created by an [omnibenevolent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omnibenevolent "Omnibenevolent") and [omnipotent](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Omnipotent "Omnipotent") being can contain suffering.[^250] As part of this tradition, Weber was careful in his study of the subject.[^251] Rather than interpreting it through a theological or ethical lens, he interpreted it through a social one.[^252] Furthermore, he incorporated [Friedrich Nietzsche](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Friedrich_Nietzsche "Friedrich Nietzsche") 's concept of *[ressentiment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ressentiment "Ressentiment")* into his discussion of the topic. However, Weber disagreed with Nietzsche's emotional discussion of *ressentiment* and his interpretation of it as a Judaism-derived expression of [slave morality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master%E2%80%93slave_morality "Master–slave morality").[^253] Weber divided theodicy into dualism, the belief that a Manichean divine struggle caused misfortune; [karma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karma "Karma"), the belief that misfortune was karmic; and predestination, the belief that only a chosen few will be saved from damnation.[^254]

Weber defined the importance of societal class within religion by examining the difference between the theodicies of fortune and misfortune and to what class structures they apply.[^255] The theodicy of fortune related to successful people's desire to prove that they deserved it. They were also prone to not being satisfied with what they already had and wished to avoid the notion that they were illegitimate or sinful.[^256] Those who were unsuccessful in life believed in the theodicy of misfortune, believing wealth and happiness would later be divinely granted to those who deserved it.[^257] Another example of how this belief of religious theodicy influenced class was that those of lower economic status tended towards deep religiousness and faith as a way to comfort themselves and provide hope for a more prosperous future, while those of higher economic status preferred the sacraments or actions that proved their right to possess greater wealth.[^258]

### The state, politics, and government

Weber defined the [state](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/State_\(polity\) "State (polity)") as an entity that had a [monopoly on violence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monopoly_on_violence "Monopoly on violence"). This meant that the state could legitimately use force to preserve itself within a given area.[^259] He also proposed that politics was the sharing of state power and influencing the distribution thereof between states and between groups within a state.[^260] Weber's definition of a politician required that they have passion, judgement, and responsibility.[^261] He divided action into the oppositional *[gesinnungsethik](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ethic_of_ultimate_ends "Ethic of ultimate ends")* and *[verantwortungsethik](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Verantwortungsethik&action=edit&redlink=1 "Verantwortungsethik (page does not exist)")* (the "ethic of ultimate ends" and the "ethic of responsibility").[^262] A *verantwortungsethik* adherent justified their actions through their consequences. Meanwhile, a *gesinnungsethik* adherent justified their actions through their ideals.[^263] While Weber thought that an ideal politician possessed both of them, he associated them with different types of people and mindsets.[^264] Combining them required that a politician be passionate about their goals and pragmatic about achieving them.[^265]

In his view, all authority-based relationships could be classified according to his [tripartite classification of authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripartite_classification_of_authority "Tripartite classification of authority").[^266] Charismatic authority was held by individuals who held power through their [charisma](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charisma "Charisma"), meaning that their power originated from extraordinary personal qualities. It was unstable, as it resisted institutionalisation and relied on the leader's success.[^267] Over time, it was forced to be routinised into more structured forms of authority. The charismatic leader's followers would create an administrative structure.[^268] Traditional authority was based on loyalty to preestablished traditions and those who held authority as a result of those traditions.[^269] For Weber, patriarchalism – the rule by a patriarch over a family – was the most important variety of traditional authority.[^270] Patrimonialism – a closely related concept to patriarchalism – was a type of traditional authority where rulers treated the government and military as extensions of their households.[^271] Rational-legal authority relied on bureaucracy and belief in both the legality of the society's rules and the legitimacy of those who held power as a result of those rules.[^272] Unlike the other types of authority, it developed gradually. That was the result of legal systems ability to exist without charismatic individuals or traditions.[^273] In *The City*, a portion of *Economy and Society* on the European city's development in [antiquity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Classical_antiquity "Classical antiquity") and the [Middle Ages](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Middle_Ages "Middle Ages"), Weber argued that a unique form of non-legitimate domination arose in medieval European cities that successfully challenged the forms of legitimate domination that had previously prevailed in the medieval world.[^274] These cities were previously under several different entities' jurisdiction that was removed as they became autonomous. That was caused by newer cities being granted privileges and the usurpation of authority in older ones.[^275]

#### Bureaucracy

Weber's commentary on societal bureaucratisation is one of the most prominent parts of his work.[^276] According to him, bureaucracy was the most efficient societal organisation method and the most formally rational system. It was necessary for modern society to function and would be difficult to destroy.[^277] Bureaucratic officials felt superior to non-bureaucrats, had a strong sense of duty, and had fixed salaries that disinclined them to pursue monetary acquisition. Bureaucracy was less likely to be found among elected officials.[^278] It was also a requirement for both modern capitalism and modern socialism to exist.[^279] Furthermore, Bureaucracy's impersonal treatment of all people suited capitalism well.[^280] This depersonalisation related to its increased efficiency. Bureaucrats could not openly make arbitrary decisions or base them on personal favours.[^281] As the most efficient and rational way of organising, bureaucratisation was the key part of rational-legal authority. Furthermore, he saw it as the key process in the ongoing rationalisation of Western society.[^282]

Weber's ideal type of bureaucracy was characterised by hierarchical organisation, delineated lines of authority in a fixed area of activity, action taken on the basis of written rules, bureaucratic officials needing expert training, rules being implemented neutrally, and career advancement depending on organisation-judged technical qualifications.[^283] The development of [communication](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Information_and_communications_technology "Information and communications technology") and transportation technologies made more efficient administration possible and popularly requested. Meanwhile, the [democratisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Democratisation "Democratisation") and rationalisation of culture resulted in demands that the new system treat everyone equally.[^284] In describing bureaucracy's history, Weber discussed multiple civilisations and said that the [Egyptian New Kingdom](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Egyptian_New_Kingdom "Egyptian New Kingdom") 's bureaucracy was the historical model for all later ones.[^285] While arguing that bureaucracy was the most efficient form of organisation and was indispensable for the modern state, Weber was also critical of it. In his view, an inescapable bureaucratisation of society would happen in the future. He also thought that a hypothetical victory of socialism over capitalism would have not prevented that.[^286] Economic and political organisations needed entrepreneurs and politicians in order to counteract bureaucrats. Otherwise, they would be stifled by bureaucracy.[^279]

#### Social stratification

Weber also formulated a three-component theory of stratification containing the elements of class, [status](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_status "Social status"), and party.[^287] Class was based on an economically determined relationship with the market, status (*Stand*) was based on non-economic qualities such as honour and prestige, and party was based on political affiliations.[^287] This distinction was most clearly described in his essay "The Distribution of Power Within the *Gemeinschaft*: Classes, *Stände*, Parties", which was first published in his book *Economy and Society*.[^288] Status served as one of the central ways of societal ranking. Issues of honour and prestige were important to it.[^289] With regards to class, the theory placed heavy emphasis on [class conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_conflict "Class conflict") and [private property](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Private_property "Private property") in its definition.[^290] While Weber drew upon Marx's interpretation of [class conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Class_conflict "Class conflict") in his definition of class, he did not see it as defining all social relations and stratification.[^291] Political parties were not given as much attention by Weber as the other two components were, as he thought that their actions were not particularly effectual. Their purpose was to seek power to benefit their members materially or ideologically.[^292]

Weber's concept of status emerged from his farm labour and the stock exchange studies, as he found social relationships that were unexplainable through economic class alone. The [Junkers](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Junker_\(Prussia\) "Junker (Prussia)") had social rules regarding marriage between different social levels and farm labourers had a strong sense of independence, neither of which was economically based.[^293] Weber maintained a sharp distinction between the terms "status" and "class", although non-scholars tend to use them interchangeably in casual use.[^294] Status and its focus on honour emerged from the *[Gemeinschaft](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gemeinschaft_and_Gesellschaft "Gemeinschaft and Gesellschaft")*, which denoted the part of society where loyalty originated from. Class emerged from the *Gesellschaft*, a subdivision of the *Gemeinschaft* that included rationally driven markets and legal organisations. Parties emerged from a combination of the two.[^295] Weber interpreted [life chances](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Life_chances "Life chances"), the opportunities to improve one's life, as a definitional aspect of class. They related to the differences in access to opportunities that individuals might have had in their lives.[^296] The relationship between status and class was not straightforward. One of them could lead to the other, and an individual or group could succeed in one but not the other.[^297]

### The vocation lectures

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/6/66/Max_Weber_-_Wissenschaft_als_Beruf_-_Seite_01.jpg/250px-Max_Weber_-_Wissenschaft_als_Beruf_-_Seite_01.jpg) ![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/8/87/Max_Weber_-_Politik_als_Beruf_Seite_01.jpg/250px-Max_Weber_-_Politik_als_Beruf_Seite_01.jpg)

1919 title pages of "Science as a Vocation" and "Politics as a Vocation"

Towards the end of his life, Weber gave two lectures, "Science as a Vocation" and "Politics as a Vocation", at [LMU Munich](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/LMU_Munich "LMU Munich") on the subjects of the scientific and political vocations. The Free Student Youth, a left-liberal student organisation, had their spokesperson, [Immanuel Birnbaum](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Immanuel_Birnbaum&action=edit&redlink=1 "Immanuel Birnbaum (page does not exist)"), invite him to give the lectures.[^298] In " [Science as a Vocation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Science_as_a_Vocation "Science as a Vocation") ", he argued that a scholar needed to possess an inner calling. Weber thought that only a particular type of person could be an academic, stating that the path forward in scholarship required the scholar to be methodical in their research and understand that they might not succeed. Specialisation was also an aspect of modern scholarship that a scholar needed to engage in.[^85] [Disenchantment](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Disenchantment "Disenchantment") and intellectual rationalisation were major aspects of his commentary on the scholar's role in modernity. They resulted in scholarship's value being questioned. Weber argued that scholarship could provide certainty through its starting presumptions, despite its inability to give absolute answers.[^299] Meanwhile, "Politics as a Vocation" commented on the subject of politics.[^98] Weber was responding to the early [Weimar Republic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Weimar_Republic "Weimar Republic") 's political instability. He argued that politicians had passion, judgement, and responsibility.[^300] There was also a division between conviction and responsibility. While these two concepts were sharply divided, it was possible for single individual – particularly the ideal politician – to possess both of them.[^99] He also divided legitimate authority into the three categories of [traditional](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Traditional_authority "Traditional authority"), [charismatic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charismatic_authority "Charismatic authority"), and [rational-legal](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rational-legal_authority "Rational-legal authority") authority.[^301] Towards the lecture's end, he described politics as "a slow, powerful drilling through hard boards".[^302] Ultimately, Weber thought that the political issues of his day required consistent effort to resolve, rather than the quick solutions that the students preferred.[^303]

### Law

Max Weber was a founder of [sociology of law](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Sociology_of_law "Sociology of law") and was trained in law and [legal history](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Legal_history "Legal history").[^304] According to Weber, law went through four developmental phases: law prophet-derived charismatic legal revelation, *[Honoratioren](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Honoratioren&action=edit&redlink=1 "Honoratioren (page does not exist)")* making and applying law, religious or secular powers imposing law, and trained legal professionals systematically expanding and administering law.[^305] Law prophets were charismatic authorities who created new laws consciously through their charisma.[^306] Meanwhile, *Honoratioren* were people who had high enough statuses to be respected in their communities while simultaneously having high enough class positions to be economically able to devote their full attention to politics.[^307] Weber argued that lawmaking as law creation and law-finding as the contextual application of such laws were complimentary. Both were potentially irrational or rational and formal or substantive.[^308] In terms of sociology of law, Weber argued that it was different than [jurisprudence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Jurisprudence "Jurisprudence") in that the former sought to understand the effects that legal rules had on social behaviour, while the latter sought to understand the rules' internal logic.[^309] He also argued that modern law was defined by legal norms' that were divided between prescriptive or prohibitory claims and permissive privileges. Claims made the keeping of promises more certain, and privileges facilitated the creation of new laws.[^310] For Weber, law was a group of conventions that were staff-enforced.[^311] He considered legal systems' development to have been indispensable for modern capitalism's own development.[^312]

### Economics

Weber primarily considered himself an economist, and all of his professorial appointments were in economics, but his contributions to that field were largely overshadowed by his role as a founder of modern sociology.[^313] He was a founder of [economic sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_sociology "Economic sociology").[^314] He described capitalism as an economic system where individuals used property as an object of trade for profit-making enterprises in a market economy and divided it into multiple categories.[^315] [Political capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_capitalism "Political capitalism") was an ancient form of capitalism where the state created opportunities for profit-making, while rational capitalism was a modern Western form of capitalism that was defined by rationality and its orientation towards markets.[^316] As a [political economist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Political_economy "Political economy") and [economic historian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_history "Economic history"), Weber belonged to the German [historical school of economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_school_of_economics "Historical school of economics"), represented by academics such as [Gustav von Schmoller](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gustav_von_Schmoller "Gustav von Schmoller") and Schmoller's student [Werner Sombart](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Werner_Sombart "Werner Sombart").[^317] While Weber's research interests were largely in line with this school, his views on methodology and [marginal utility](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marginal_utility "Marginal utility") significantly diverged from them. Instead, they were closer to those of [Carl Menger](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Menger "Carl Menger") and the [Austrian school of economics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austrian_school_of_economics "Austrian school of economics"), the historical school's traditional rivals.[^318] Unlike other [historicists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historicist "Historicist"), Weber accepted marginal utility and taught it to his students.[^319] In 1908, Weber published an article, "Marginal Utility Theory and 'The Fundamental Law of Psychophysics'", arguing that marginal utility and economics were not based on [psychophysics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Psychophysics "Psychophysics").[^320] In general, Weber disagreed with the idea that economics relied on another field.[^321] The division caused by the *[Methodenstreit](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Methodenstreit "Methodenstreit")* caused Weber to support a broad interpretation of economics that combined economic theory, economic history, and economic sociology in the form of *[Sozialökonomik](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Sozial%C3%B6konomik&action=edit&redlink=1 "Sozialökonomik (page does not exist)")* (social economics).[^322]

### Economy and Society

![A page from the Economy and Society manuscript](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/0/0b/Manuskript_max_weber_rechtssoziologie.jpg/250px-Manuskript_max_weber_rechtssoziologie.jpg)

A page from the manuscript of the sociology of law within Economy and Society

Weber's *magnum opus* *Economy and Society* is an essay collection that he was working on at the time of his death in 1920.[^323] It included a wide range of essays dealing with Weber's views regarding sociology, [social philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_philosophy "Social philosophy"), politics, [social stratification](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_stratification "Social stratification"), [world religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/World_religions "World religions"), diplomacy, and other subjects.[^324] The text was largely unfinished, outside of the first three chapters. These chapters were written between 1919 and 1920.[^325] They relate to *verstehende* sociology, [economic sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economic_sociology "Economic sociology"), authority, and class and status groups, respectively.[^326] The first chapter is one of the two most frequently consulted portions of the text by Weberian scholars – alongside the third chapter – and moved from a discussion of individuals' [social actions](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_action "Social action") to the centrality of the [monopoly on violence](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Monopoly_on_violence "Monopoly on violence") to the state.[^327] Meanwhile, the second chapter is less discussed, as it represented a type of economic theory that had fallen out of style amongst economists after the 1930s.[^328] It presented an analytical economic history that contained a discussion of the origins of capitalism and argued that the [Austrian](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Austrian_school_of_economics "Austrian school of economics") and [historical schools](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Historical_school_of_economics "Historical school of economics") of economics could be methodologically synthesised.[^329] Chapter three focused on [Weber's tripartite definition of legitimate authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Tripartite_classification_of_authority "Tripartite classification of authority").[^330] Since the final chapter was unfinished, it was largely a brief set of classifications of classes and *Stände* (statuses).[^331]

After Weber's death, the final organisation and editing of the book fell to his widow [Marianne](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marianne_Weber "Marianne Weber"). She was assisted by the economist [Melchior Palyi](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Melchior_Palyi "Melchior Palyi"). The resulting volume was published in four volumes from February 1921 to September 1922 and was titled *Wirtschaft und Gesellschaft*.[^332] It also included additional texts that were generally written between 1909 and 1914 that his widow found amongst his belongings.[^333] In 1956, the German jurist [Johannes Winckelmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Johannes_Winckelmann&action=edit&redlink=1 "Johannes Winckelmann (page does not exist)") edited and organised a revised fourth edition of *Economy and Society*, later editing a fifth edition in 1976.[^334] [Guenther Roth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Guenther_Roth "Guenther Roth") and Claus Wittich edited an English translation of the work in 1968. It was based on a 1964 revised edition of Winckelmann's version of the text.[^335] The *[Max Weber-Gesamtausgabe](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Max_Weber-Gesamtausgabe&action=edit&redlink=1 "Max Weber-Gesamtausgabe (page does not exist)")* editors published *Economy and Society* in six parts, with the first devoted to the first four chapters thereof. The remaining five parts were organised in chronological order based on when they were written.[^336] In 2019, Keith Tribe published a revised English translation of the *Max Weber-Gesamtausgabe* edition of its first four chapters.[^337]

## Inspirations

![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/7/79/Immanuel_Kant_-_Gemaelde_1.jpg/250px-Immanuel_Kant_-_Gemaelde_1.jpg) ![](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/a/a2/Marx_old.jpg/250px-Marx_old.jpg)

Immanuel Kant and Karl Marx, two of Max Weber's influences

Weber was strongly influenced by [German idealism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/German_idealism "German idealism"), particularly the [neo-Kantianism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Neo-Kantianism "Neo-Kantianism") of the Southwestern School.[^338] [Heinrich Rickert](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Heinrich_Rickert "Heinrich Rickert") and [Wilhelm Windelband](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelm_Windelband "Wilhelm Windelband") were significant figures in this school.[^339] He understood [Immanuel Kant](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Immanuel_Kant "Immanuel Kant") through [moral psychology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Moral_psychology "Moral psychology") and [philosophical anthropology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophical_anthropology "Philosophical anthropology").[^340] Weber's methodology and ethics were also influenced by him.[^341] In terms of methodology, he used the neo-Kantian conceptual framework to articulate his own concepts.[^342] Weber was also influenced by [Kantian ethics](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Kantian_ethics "Kantian ethics") in that he ascribed to the categorical imperative and sought non-arbitrary freedom.[^343] However, he considered Kantian ethics to be obsolete in a modern age without religious certainties.[^344] His responses to the questions posed by Kant and neo-Kantianism were pessimistic as a result.[^345]

Weber was responding to [Friedrich Nietzsche's philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Philosophy_of_Friedrich_Nietzsche "Philosophy of Friedrich Nietzsche") 's effect on modern thought. His goal in the field of ethics was to find non-arbitrarily defined freedom in what he interpreted as a post-metaphysical age, representing a division between the Kantian and Nietzschean parts of his thought.[^340] After his 1920 debate with [Oswald Spengler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Spengler "Oswald Spengler"), Weber said that the world was significantly intellectually influenced by Nietzsche and Marx.[^346] In *The Protestant Work Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* and "Science as a Vocation", Weber negatively described and warned about " *die 'letzten Menschen'* " ("the 'last men'"), who were Nietzschean "specialists without spirit".[^347] He also used Nietzsche's concept of *ressentiment* in his discussion of theodicy, but disliked Nietzsche's emotional approach to it and did not interpret it as Judaism-derived [slave morality](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Master%E2%80%93slave_morality "Master–slave morality").[^348]

[Karl Marx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx "Karl Marx") 's writings were also a major influence on Weber's works.[^349] While Weber agreed with Marx on [social conflict](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_conflict "Social conflict") 's importance, he did not think that a society would be destroyed if the traditions that upheld it were valued more than it was. Furthermore, he thought that a social conflict could be resolved within the preexisting [social system](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_system "Social system").[^350] Writing in 1932, [Karl Löwith](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_L%C3%B6with "Karl Löwith") contrasted Marx and Weber, arguing that both were interested in Western [capitalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Capitalism "Capitalism") 's causes and effects, but viewed it through different lenses. Marx interpreted it through [alienation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Marx%27s_theory_of_alienation "Marx's theory of alienation"), while Weber interpreted it through [rationalisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Rationalization_\(sociology\) "Rationalization (sociology)").[^351] Weber also expanded Marx's alienation theory from workers who were alienated from their work to similar situations involving intellectuals and bureaucrats.[^352] [Cold War era](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Cold_War_era "Cold War era") scholars frequently interpreted Weber as "a bourgeois answer to Marx", but he was instead responding the [Wilhelmine](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wilhelminism "Wilhelminism") [bourgeoisie](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Bourgeoisie "Bourgeoisie") 's issues. In that regard, he focused on rationality and irrationality's conflict.[^353]

## Legacy

Alongside [Karl Marx](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Karl_Marx "Karl Marx") and [Émile Durkheim](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/%C3%89mile_Durkheim "Émile Durkheim"), Weber is commonly regarded as one of modern sociology's founders.[^354] He was instrumental in developing an [antipositivist](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Antipositivist "Antipositivist"), [hermeneutic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hermeneutic "Hermeneutic"), tradition in the [social sciences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_science "Social science").[^355] Weber influenced many scholars across the political spectrum.[^356] Left-leaning [social theorists](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Social_theorist "Social theorist") – such as [Theodor W. Adorno](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Theodor_W._Adorno "Theodor W. Adorno"), [Max Horkheimer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Horkheimer "Max Horkheimer"), [György Lukács](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Gy%C3%B6rgy_Luk%C3%A1cs "György Lukács"), and [Jürgen Habermas](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/J%C3%BCrgen_Habermas "Jürgen Habermas") – were influenced by his discussion of [modernity](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernity "Modernity") and its friction with [modernisation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Modernisation "Modernisation").[^357] Furthermore, his analysis of modernity and rationalisation significantly influenced the [Frankfurt School](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Frankfurt_School "Frankfurt School") 's [critical theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Critical_theory "Critical theory").[^358] Right-leaning scholars – including [Carl Schmitt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Carl_Schmitt "Carl Schmitt"), [Joseph Schumpeter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Joseph_Schumpeter "Joseph Schumpeter"), [Leo Strauss](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leo_Strauss "Leo Strauss"), [Hans Morgenthau](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Morgenthau "Hans Morgenthau"), and [Raymond Aron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Aron "Raymond Aron") – focused on his discussion of strong democratic leaders, political ethics' relationship with value-freedom and value-relativism, and combating bureaucracy through political action.[^340] The scholars who examined his works philosophically, including Strauss, [Hans Henrik Bruun](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Henrik_Bruun "Hans Henrik Bruun"), and [Alfred Schütz](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Sch%C3%BCtz "Alfred Schütz"), traditionally looked at them through the lens of [Continental philosophy](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Continental_philosophy "Continental philosophy").[^359]

### Weber studies

Weberian scholarship's beginnings were delayed by the disruption of academic life in the Weimar Republic. Hyper-inflation caused Weber's support for parliamentary democracy to be countered by the declining respect that German professors had for it.[^360] Their political alienation caused many of them to become pessimistic and closer to the historical viewpoints espoused by [Oswald Spengler](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Oswald_Spengler "Oswald Spengler") in his *[The Decline of the West](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Decline_of_the_West "The Decline of the West")*.[^361] Furthermore, universities were increasingly under state control and influence, which accelerated after the [Nazi Party](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Party "Nazi Party") took power. The previously dominant style of sociology, that of [Alfred Vierkandt](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Alfred_Vierkandt "Alfred Vierkandt") and [Leopold von Wiese](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Leopold_von_Wiese "Leopold von Wiese"), was largely replaced by a sociology that was dominated by Nazis. [Hans Freyer](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Freyer "Hans Freyer") and [Othmar Spann](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Othmar_Spann "Othmar Spann") were representative of that movement, while Werner Sombart began to support collectivism and Nazism.[^362] The Nazi Party's rise marginalised Weber's scholarship in the German academy.[^363] However, some Weberian scholars left Germany, with most of them settling in the United States and the United Kingdom.[^364]

![A moustached bald man reclining in a chair in front of a bookshelf.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/9/95/TalcottParsons.jpg/250px-TalcottParsons.jpg)

Talcott Parsons c. 1960

These scholars entered American and British academia when English translations of Weber's writings, such as the *General Economic History*, were beginning to be published.[^365] [Talcott Parsons](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Talcott_Parsons "Talcott Parsons"), an American scholar, was influenced by his readings of Weber and Sombart as a student in 1920s Germany.[^366] Marianne Weber gave him permission to publish a *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism* translation in his 1930 essay collection, the *[Collected Essays on the Sociology of Religion](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Collected_Essays_on_the_Sociology_of_Religion&action=edit&redlink=1 "Collected Essays on the Sociology of Religion (page does not exist)")*.[^367] It was heavily edited by the publisher and was not initially successful.[^368] Nevertheless, it was part of Parsons's effort to create an academic sociology, which resulted in his 1937 book *[The Structure of Social Action](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/The_Structure_of_Social_Action "The Structure of Social Action")*.[^369] In it, Parsons argued that Weber and Durkheim were foundational sociologists.[^370] However, his book was not successful until after the [Second World War](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Second_World_War "Second World War").[^371] He translated *[Economy and Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Economy_and_Society "Economy and Society")* as *The Theory of Social and Economic Organization*.[^372] Parsons's increasing scholarly prominence increased its influence.[^371] Other translations began to appear, including [C. Wright Mills](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/C._Wright_Mills "C. Wright Mills") and [Hans Gerth](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Hans_Gerth "Hans Gerth") 's *[From Max Weber: Essays in Sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=From_Max_Weber:_Essays_in_Sociology&action=edit&redlink=1 "From Max Weber: Essays in Sociology (page does not exist)")* in 1946, which was a collection of excerpts from Weber's writings.[^373] In 1949, [Edward Shils](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Edward_Shils "Edward Shils") edited a translation of Weber's *[Collected Essays on Methodology](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Collected_Essays_on_Methodology&action=edit&redlink=1 "Collected Essays on Methodology (page does not exist)")*, which was published as *The Methodology of the Social Sciences*.[^374]

As the 1940s ended, Weber's scholarly reputation rose due to scholarly interpretations of it through the lenses of Parsons's [structural functionalism](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Structural_functionalism "Structural functionalism") and Mills's [conflict theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Conflict_theory "Conflict theory").[^375] Over the course of the following decades, continued translations of Weber's works began to appear, including ones on law, religion, music, and the city. Despite the translations' flaws, it became possible to obtain a largely complete view of Weber's scholarship. That was still impeded by the translations' unorganised publication, which prevented scholars from knowing the texts' connections.[^376] In 1968, a complete translation of Marianne Weber's prepared version of *Economy and Society* was published.[^377] While *From Max Weber* first forwarded an interpretation of Weber that was separate from Parson's structural functionalism, a more political and historical interpretation was forwarded by [Reinhard Bendix](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Reinhard_Bendix "Reinhard Bendix") 's 1948 *[Max Weber: An Intellectual Portrait](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Max_Weber:_An_Intellectual_Portrait&action=edit&redlink=1 "Max Weber: An Intellectual Portrait (page does not exist)")*, [Ralf Dahrendorf](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Ralf_Dahrendorf "Ralf Dahrendorf") 's 1957 *[Class and Conflict in an Industrial Society](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Class_and_Conflict_in_an_Industrial_Society&action=edit&redlink=1 "Class and Conflict in an Industrial Society (page does not exist)")*, and [John Rex](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/John_Rex "John Rex") 's 1962 *[Key Problems in Sociological Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Key_Problems_in_Sociological_Theory&action=edit&redlink=1 "Key Problems in Sociological Theory (page does not exist)")*.[^378] Meanwhile, the 1964 centennial conference on Max Weber was held in Heidelberg.[^379] It featured a controversy over whether Weber's concept of [charismatic authority](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Charismatic_authority "Charismatic authority") played a role in [Nazi Germany](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Nazi_Germany "Nazi Germany") 's rise.[^380] A year later, [Raymond Aron](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Raymond_Aron "Raymond Aron") 's *[Main Currents in Sociological Thought](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Main_Currents_in_Sociological_Thought&action=edit&redlink=1 "Main Currents in Sociological Thought (page does not exist)")* gave an alternative to Parson's perspective on the [history of sociology](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/History_of_sociology "History of sociology") and framed Weber, Marx, and Durkheim as the three foundational figures. [Anthony Giddens](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Anthony_Giddens "Anthony Giddens") 's 1971 *[Capitalism and Modern Social Theory](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Capitalism_and_Modern_Social_Theory&action=edit&redlink=1 "Capitalism and Modern Social Theory (page does not exist)")* solidified that interpretation. After the 1970s, more of Weber's less prominent publications were published.[^378] This coincided with the continued writing of critical commentaries on his works and ideas, including the creation of a scholarly journal in 2000 – *[Max Weber Studies](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Max_Weber_Studies&action=edit&redlink=1 "Max Weber Studies (page does not exist)")* – that is devoted to such scholarship.[^381] Additionally, the [Max Weber Foundation](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Max_Weber_Foundation "Max Weber Foundation") was founded in 2002 as the managing body for the German Institutes of the Humanities.[^382] Weber's death centenary in 2020 provoked several [scholarly conferences](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Scholarly_conference "Scholarly conference") that were held online as a result of the [COVID-19 pandemic](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/COVID-19_pandemic "COVID-19 pandemic").[^383]

### Max Weber-Gesamtausgabe

![A blue book cover.](https://upload.wikimedia.org/wikipedia/commons/thumb/d/d8/MWG_I_22-1_Buchdeckel.JPG/250px-MWG_I_22-1_Buchdeckel.JPG)

The cover of the first part of the twenty-second volume of the Max Weber-Gesamtausgabe

The idea of publishing a collected edition of Weber's complete works was pushed forward by [Horst Baier](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Horst_Baier&action=edit&redlink=1 "Horst Baier (page does not exist)") in 1972. A year later, the *[Max Weber-Gesamtausgabe](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Max_Weber-Gesamtausgabe&action=edit&redlink=1 "Max Weber-Gesamtausgabe (page does not exist)")*, a multi-volume set of all of his writings, began to take shape.[^384] [Wolfgang J. Mommsen](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Mommsen "Wolfgang Mommsen"), [Wolfgang Schluchter](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Wolfgang_Schluchter "Wolfgang Schluchter"), [Johannes Winckelmann](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Johannes_Winckelmann&action=edit&redlink=1 "Johannes Winckelmann (page does not exist)"), [M. Rainer Lepsius](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/M._Rainer_Lepsius "M. Rainer Lepsius"), and Horst Baier were the initial editors.[^385] [Mohr Siebeck](https://en.wikipedia.org/wiki/Mohr_Siebeck "Mohr Siebeck") was selected as the publisher.[^386] The project was presented to the academic community in 1981 with the publication of a prospectus that was colloquially referred to as the "green brochure". It outlined the series' three sections: "Writings and Speeches", "Letters", and "Lecture Manuscripts and Lecture Notes".[^387] Four years later, the project entered publication.[^388] The writings were organised in a combination of chronological order and by subject, with material that Weber did not intended for publication in purely chronological order.[^389] The final editions of each text were used, aside from *The Protestant Ethic and the Spirit of Capitalism*, which was included in both its originally published and revised forms.[^390] After Mommsen's death in 2004, [Gangolf Hübinger](https://en.wikipedia.org/w/index.php?title=Gangolf_H%C3%BCbinger&action=edit&redlink=1 "Gangolf Hübinger (page does not exist)") succeeded him.[^391] Winckelmann, Lepsius, and Baier also died before its completion.[^392] It concluded in June 2020 and contains forty-seven volumes, including two index volumes.[^393]
